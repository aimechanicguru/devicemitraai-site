Air conditioner safety guide

AC trips MCB when switched on

Repeated MCB tripping can indicate an electrical fault, compressor issue, water-related short, wiring problem, or overload. Treat it as a safety signal, not a normal reset problem.

Safety class: S2-S3

Immediate safety action

Do not repeatedly reset the MCB to test the AC. Keep the AC off and call a qualified AC technician or electrician if the MCB trips again, if there is burning smell, visible water near wiring, sparks, shock sensation, or outdoor-unit wiring damage.

Safe observations to record

  • Does the MCB trip immediately, after a few seconds, or after the compressor starts?
  • Does it happen only in cooling mode, or even when the AC is switched on without cooling?
  • Is there burning smell, water leakage, visible wire damage, or recent servicing?
  • Does the outdoor unit start before the trip, or does the trip happen before any outdoor activity?
  • Does any other appliance on the same circuit also fail or dim?

Possible fault directions

Possible direction Evidence that supports it What still needs checking
Compressor or outdoor-unit fault Trip occurs when outdoor unit attempts to start. Technician inspection of compressor, capacitor, contactor, and wiring.
Short circuit or insulation problem Immediate trip, burning smell, moisture, or visible wire damage. Professional isolation and electrical testing.
Overload or weak protection device Trip after load starts, shared circuit, or old MCB. Electrician check of load, rating, wiring condition, and protection.

What DeviceMitra AI would prepare

  • Diagnosis report: possible fault categories ranked by reported evidence.
  • Repair guidance: stop-use instructions, safe observations, and technician-only checks.
  • Tools list: phone camera and flashlight for the user; multimeter, insulation testing, and AC tools for professionals.
  • Missing evidence: trip timing, outdoor-unit behavior, water or smell, and circuit sharing.

Do not do these checks as a household user

  • Do not open electrical covers or outdoor-unit panels.
  • Do not bypass the MCB or use a higher-rated MCB to stop tripping.
  • Do not touch wet wiring or metal parts if shock is suspected.
  • Do not repeatedly restart the AC after a trip.
